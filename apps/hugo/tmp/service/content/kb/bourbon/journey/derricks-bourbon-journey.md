# 🥃 Derrick's Bourbon Journey

> *"This started as trying to find a good bourbon. It became learning why I liked certain bourbons more than others."*

---

# Why I Started

About a year ago I decided to seriously learn bourbon.

Like many people, I assumed the goal was finding the rare bottles...

Buffalo Trace.

Blanton's.

Weller.

Allocated unicorns.

Instead I discovered something much more interesting.

I discovered **my own palate.**

Today I spend far less time chasing hype and much more time understanding why certain bottles consistently make me smile.

---

# The Biggest Lesson

I don't buy bourbon because someone on YouTube told me to.

I buy bourbon because I've learned what flavors I actually enjoy.

That turned bourbon hunting from a collecting hobby into a learning hobby.

---

# My Flavor DNA

After dozens of bottles and countless pours, I've learned my palate consistently prefers:

✅ Rich caramel

✅ Peanut brittle

✅ Mature oak

✅ Baking spices

✅ Cinnamon

✅ Dark cherry

✅ Orange peel

✅ Long finishes

✅ Thick, oily mouthfeel

✅ Balanced sweetness

---

Things I enjoy less:

• overly sweet finished bourbons

• sharp ethanol heat

• young harsh oak

• excessive rye spice

• bottles that feel one-dimensional

---

# The Heaven Hill Revelation

This surprised me more than anything.

Originally I assumed Buffalo Trace would become my benchmark.

Instead...

I kept coming back to Heaven Hill.

Every time.

Heaven Hill Bottled in Bond quietly became the bottle I compare everything else against.

It isn't the rarest.

It isn't the most expensive.

It simply delivers exactly what I enjoy.

Rich caramel.

Peanut brittle.

Mature oak.

Excellent mouthfeel.

Wonderful balance.

It became my bourbon north star.

---

# Discovering Bottled in Bond

Learning the Bottled in Bond Act completely changed how I shop.

Today, when I see Bottled in Bond on a label I immediately know I'm getting:

• 100 proof

• At least four years old

• One distillery

• One distilling season

• Federally supervised aging

It isn't a guarantee of greatness...

…but it is an outstanding indicator of quality and consistency.

---

# The DSP Number Rabbit Hole

One of the most enjoyable rabbit holes was learning about federal Distilled Spirits Plant (DSP) numbers.

That led me to an unexpected discovery.

## Kirkland Signature Bottled in Bond

DSP-KY-12

Bardstown, Kentucky

DSP-KY-12 belongs to Heaven Hill.

While Costco doesn't reveal the exact mash bill or age, the bottle almost certainly comes from the Heaven Hill family of distillates.

At around $25 for a one-liter bottle, it may be one of the best values on the shelf.

I don't expect it to replace Heaven Hill Bottled in Bond 7 Year.

But it's an outstanding daily drinker that shares many familiar characteristics.

# 🧪 The Experimentation Layer

These are the bottles that didn’t necessarily become favorites—but *trained the palate*.

They explain how I learned what I like.

## 🟤 The Benchmark Foundation

Wild Turkey 101  
- Early reference point  
- Sharp, honest, slightly hot  
- Set the baseline for “good bourbon taste”

Evan Williams Bottled in Bond  
- First real structured value bourbon  
- Helped define what “cheap but correct” means

---

## 🔥 Proof Calibration Tier

Elijah Craig Barrel Proof  
- Incredible depth  
- But high proof requires respect  
- Taught me that intensity ≠ daily drinkability

1792 Full Proof  
- Sweet + heat imbalance  
- Great for learning “too much of a good thing”

Larceny Barrel Proof  
- Excellent but fatiguing  
- 2–3 day recovery bottle (proof discipline lesson)

Rare Breed  
- First “high proof that still drinks easy”

---

## 🪵 Oak & Double Barrel Experiments

Old Forester 1910  
- Soft chocolate + marshmallow  
- First “dessert oak” recognition point

Old Forester 1920  
- Darker, thicker, more aggressive  
- Showed the full spectrum of OF blending

Woodford Reserve Double Oaked  
- The turning point bottle  
- Toffee, raisin, controlled oak sweetness  
- Defined “dessert bourbon standard”

Bondstone Double Oak (Port Stave Finish)  
- Unexpected restraint  
- Showed wine finish doesn’t have to dominate

---

## 🍯 Sweetness & Soft Profile Tests

Bowman Brothers Small Batch  
- Light, soft sweetness  
- Helped define what “thin but pleasant” means

---

## 🧂 Outlier / Learning Misses

Sazerac Rye  
- Grassy, herbal, sharp rye bite  
- Clear mismatch with current palate

Jack Daniel’s Old No. 7  
- Banana-nail-polish note  
- Defined “what I don’t want in bourbon”

Old Forester 100  
- Hot, light, slightly thin  
- Good value but missing viscosity

---

# 🧠 Insight

These bottles didn’t fail.

They *calibrated the system.*

Every “favorite” bottle is only a favorite because these existed first.

# Looking Ahead

One of the biggest surprises of this journey is realizing that I no longer shop for bourbon the way I did when I started.

A year ago, my shopping list was driven by hype.

Today it's driven by curiosity.

Instead of asking,

"What's allocated?"

I ask,

"What can this bottle teach me?"

Sometimes the answer comes from a $120 bottle.

Sometimes it comes from a $25 Bottled in Bond bourbon sitting on the bottom shelf.

I've learned not to underestimate either.

# Lessons Learned

If I could go back and give myself advice on day one, it would be this.

Don't chase hype.

Don't chase proof.

Don't chase age.

Don't chase allocated bottles.

Learn distilleries.

Learn mash bills.

Learn Bottled in Bond.

Learn DSP numbers.

Take notes.

Taste side by side.

Trust your palate.

And remember that price doesn't guarantee enjoyment.

Some of my favorite bottles cost less than forty dollars.

That's one of the best discoveries I've made.
