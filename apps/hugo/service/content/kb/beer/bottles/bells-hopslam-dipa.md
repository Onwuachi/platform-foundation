# Why I Bought It

Hopslam has been one of the most talked-about Double IPAs in the Midwest for years. After enjoying Bell's Two Hearted and developing a deeper appreciation for balanced beers over simply chasing high ABV or bitterness, I wanted to finally see if Hopslam lived up to its reputation.

# Appearance

Deep golden-orange with excellent clarity.

A fluffy white head leaves impressive lacing down the glass.

Looks every bit like a classic American Double IPA.

---

# Aroma

Huge citrus immediately jumps from the glass.

Orange zest, grapefruit, tropical fruit and floral hops dominate.

Behind the hops is a noticeable honey sweetness that rounds everything out instead of becoming cloying.

Despite the 10% ABV, the alcohol stays remarkably restrained on the nose.

---

# Flavor

This is a hop-forward beer, but not an aggressively bitter one.

Bright citrus leads into grapefruit, orange peel and tropical fruit before transitioning into honey sweetness and light malt.

The bitterness is firm yet controlled.

Rather than feeling like a palate assault, everything remains integrated and surprisingly drinkable for a 10% Double IPA.

The alcohol is exceptionally well hidden.

---

# Mouthfeel

Medium-full body with a smooth, slightly creamy texture.

Carbonation is lively enough to keep the beer from becoming heavy.

For a 10% beer, it drinks far lighter than expected.

---

# Finish

Long citrus and hop bitterness linger, supported by subtle honey sweetness.

The finish remains clean and encourages another sip rather than becoming harsh or overly resinous.

---

# What I Liked

- Outstanding balance for a Double IPA
- Alcohol is incredibly well hidden
- Bright citrus without becoming overly bitter
- Honey sweetness complements rather than dominates
- Dangerous drinkability for a 10% beer
- Excellent mouthfeel

---

# What I'd Change

Very little.

If anything, I'd slightly reduce the sweetness, but doing so might also remove part of what makes Hopslam unique.

# Where It Fits

Hopslam sits firmly in the "premium seasonal treat" category.

It isn't an everyday lawnmower beer, nor is it meant to be.

Instead, it's a beer to slow down and appreciate when you're in the mood for something bold, flavorful, and exceptionally well crafted.

---

# Final Thoughts

Hopslam reminds me of something I've also learned while exploring bourbon.

The best drinks aren't necessarily the biggest, strongest, or most intense.

They're the ones where every component works together.

Bell's could have made this beer aggressively bitter or overwhelmingly sweet.

Instead, they produced a remarkably balanced Double IPA that hides its 10% ABV behind citrus, honey, and careful hop integration.

That's much harder to do than simply making a big beer.

## Question Answered

**Does Hopslam deserve its reputation?**

**Yes.**

The hype is justified—not because it's the biggest Double IPA, but because it's one of the most balanced. Much like Heaven Hill 7 Year Bottled-in-Bond became my bourbon benchmark through balance rather than rarity, Hopslam demonstrates that exceptional integration is often more impressive than raw intensity.
