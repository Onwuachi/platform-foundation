# Why I Bought It

I enjoy exploring Belgian-style ales, and the addition of African honey and orange immediately caught my attention. While many Tripels stay close to traditional Belgian recipes, this one promised something a little different without abandoning the style's foundation. After several purchases, it has become one of the beers I use as a personal benchmark when evaluating other Tripels.

# Appearance

The beer pours a bright golden color with excellent clarity and a thick, white foamy head that lingers nicely. A constant stream of fine, effervescent bubbles rises through the glass, giving it an elegant presentation that hints at its Belgian heritage. Despite the 10% ABV, it looks light, inviting, and highly carbonated rather than heavy.

---

# Aroma

The aroma immediately showcases expressive Belgian yeast, followed by notes of African honey, floral sweetness, ripe fig, and sweet graham cracker. As the beer warms, subtle hints of clove, light pepper, banana, and citrus peel become more noticeable, creating a layered aroma that continues to evolve throughout the glass.

---

# Flavor

The flavor is exceptionally well balanced. African honey provides a smooth, natural sweetness while orange adds brightness without becoming artificial or candy-like. The Belgian yeast remains the foundation of the beer, contributing subtle spice and fruit character throughout.

Bitterness stays surprisingly restrained for a beer of this strength, allowing the honey, malt, and yeast to remain the stars. The 10% ABV is remarkably well hidden, making this one of those beers that drinks much easier than its alcohol content suggests.

---

# Mouthfeel

The body falls between medium and full with a creamy, almost silky texture. Lively carbonation keeps the beer feeling refreshing without becoming sharp or overly fizzy. Although there is a slight hop bitterness, it never overpowers the malt or honey character. The beer remains malt-forward while avoiding the heaviness that can make stronger ales feel syrupy.

Its smooth texture and excellent balance make it surprisingly easy to drink despite the high alcohol content.

---

# Finish

The finish is clean and pleasantly long, leaving lingering notes of honey sweetness, orange citrus, gentle Belgian spice, and a touch of pepper. A subtle warming sensation from the alcohol reminds you of its strength without becoming harsh, while the finish dries out enough to encourage another sip.

---

# What I Liked

This has become one of my benchmark Tripels.

What separates it from many others I've tried is its balance. The African honey and orange aren't gimmicks—they complement the traditional Belgian yeast profile instead of competing with it. The result is a beer that feels both approachable and distinctly Belgian.

I also appreciate that it isn't overly expensive. At around **$12.99 for a six-pack**, it delivers outstanding value for a well-crafted 10% Belgian-style ale. It's a beer I can enjoy year-round rather than saving only for special occasions.

One of the biggest surprises is how much better it becomes as it warms. I actually prefer drinking it above **50°F**, where the honey, yeast, and citrus become far more expressive than when served refrigerator cold.

If I were introducing someone to Belgian-style Tripels—and I knew they were comfortable with higher-alcohol beers—this would likely be the bottle I'd hand them first. It is smooth, balanced, flavorful, and easy to appreciate without feeling overly complex or pretentious.

---

# What I'd Change

Honestly, not much.

If I had one criticism, it would simply be wider availability. It's a beer I'd happily keep stocked year-round because it offers excellent value and consistently delivers an enjoyable drinking experience.

The additional warmth unlocks layers of honey, citrus, and Belgian yeast that are easy to miss when the beer is served too cold.

For me, this has become one of the benchmark beers in the style. It combines authentic Belgian character with unique African honey and orange additions that enhance rather than distract.
