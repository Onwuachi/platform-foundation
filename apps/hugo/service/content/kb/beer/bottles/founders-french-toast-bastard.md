# Why I Bought It

I picked up Founders French Toast Bastard because the concept immediately caught my attention. A high-ABV Scotch Ale built around vanilla, cinnamon, and maple sounded like it could easily go too far into dessert-beer territory.

Instead, it lands in a surprisingly controlled place.

This is a beer that drinks like a **morning stout**—rich, thick, sweet, and warming, with the flavors of French toast layered over a dark malt foundation.

# Appearance

The beer appears almost like a **toasted stout**.

It isn't quite as dark and opaque as a traditional Russian or Imperial Stout, instead leaning slightly more toward a deep brown appearance with a rich, toasted character.

Visually, it immediately suggests something heavy and malt-forward without looking quite as intense as a classic black Imperial Stout.

---

# Aroma

The aroma brings the expected French toast profile without letting any single component completely dominate.

Vanilla, cinnamon, and maple are all present, but they remain relatively balanced.

The result is inviting and distinctly breakfast-like.

It smells like a beer that could legitimately be described as a **morning stout**—even though technically it is a Scotch Ale.

---

# Flavor

The vanilla, cinnamon, and maple combination is noticeable but **not overpowering**.

That is probably my favorite part of the beer.

The maple provides sweetness, while cinnamon and vanilla give it that French toast character without turning the beer into straight-up syrup.

It is sweet, but it stays on the controlled side of the line.

This is where I think Founders got the balance right.

For comparison, I could easily see myself wishing a beer like Knob Creek Maple had dialed its sweetness back to approximately this level.

French Toast Bastard gives you the flavor without completely overwhelming the underlying beer.

---

# Mouthfeel

Thick.

Viscous.

Rich.

This is a substantial beer.

The texture matches the 11.1% ABV and the overall flavor profile. It has enough body to support the vanilla, cinnamon, and maple without feeling thin or watered down.

The viscosity also contributes heavily to the dessert-like experience, but it never becomes completely unmanageable.

---

# Finish

The finish is warm, sweet, and lingering.

The maple and cinnamon remain present while the alcohol provides a noticeable warming effect.

The sweetness comes close to the edge but doesn't quite fall off the cliff into a full-on sugar high.

That restraint is important.

For a beer built around French toast flavors, it could have easily become excessive. Instead, the sweetness remains relatively controlled.

---

# What I Liked

* Vanilla, cinnamon, and maple are all noticeable without one flavor completely taking over.
* Thick and viscous mouthfeel.
* Sweet without becoming overwhelmingly sugary.
* Strong French toast character.
* Feels like a "morning stout" despite being a Scotch Ale.
* 11.1% ABV provides a substantial warming character.
* The sweetness is better controlled than some maple-forward beers.
* Dessert-like without completely abandoning the underlying malt character.

---

# What I'd Change

The biggest limitation is personal preference.

As good as this beer is, I'm still fundamentally a **traditional Russian/Imperial Stout guy**.

I enjoy the richness and intensity of a classic Imperial Stout—the dark roasted malt, chocolate, coffee, and deep malt complexity.

French Toast Bastard is doing something different, and it does that thing very well.

But if I'm choosing my ideal beer style, I will probably still reach for the traditional Imperial Stout.

# Aging & Bottle Details

**Bottled:** August 15, 2023
**Tasted:** July 21, 2026
**Approximate Bottle Age:** 2 years, 11 months

**Label Classification:** Malt beverage with natural flavors — ale aged in oak bourbon barrels with vanilla extract, cinnamon, and maple extract.

This bottle had nearly three years of age by the time I opened it.

That makes the balance of the beer even more interesting. Despite the long time in the bottle, the vanilla, cinnamon, and maple character remained noticeable without becoming overwhelming or turning the beer into a syrup bomb.

The bourbon barrel aging also adds another layer to the experience, contributing warmth and depth behind the sweet breakfast-inspired flavors.

The result is still very much a dessert-leaning beer, but the age may have helped the individual components settle into a more integrated profile.

For my palate, this is probably part of why the sweetness works as well as it does. The beer is undeniably sweet, thick, and viscous, but it doesn't completely overwhelm the malt and barrel character.

At nearly three years old, this bottle showed that the beer can carry substantial age while maintaining its identity.

That said, I would be curious to compare this directly against a fresher bottle. It would be interesting to see whether the vanilla, cinnamon, and maple were originally more aggressive and have mellowed with age, or whether this was always intended to be a relatively restrained flavor profile.

---

# Final Thoughts

Founders French Toast Bastard is a very well-executed flavored Scotch Ale.

The combination of vanilla, cinnamon, and maple gives it a distinct French toast character, but the flavors are surprisingly restrained.

The beer is thick, viscous, sweet, and warming without crossing completely into sugar-bomb territory.

In fact, the sweetness level is one of its strengths.

I could easily see myself wishing other maple-forward beers would dial their sweetness back to this level.

That said, my personal preference still lies with the traditional Russian and Imperial Stout category. I prefer the deeper roasted malt character, chocolate, coffee, and dark complexity those beers typically bring.

But judged on what French Toast Bastard is trying to accomplish, this is a success.

It is a **morning stout in spirit**—rich, warming, indulgent, and breakfast-inspired—without becoming completely ridiculous.

---

# Verdict

**8.7 / 10**

A thick, viscous, high-ABV Scotch Ale with a surprisingly well-controlled French toast profile. Vanilla, cinnamon, and maple are present without overwhelming the beer, and the sweetness stops just short of becoming excessive.

Not my preferred style over a traditional Russian or Imperial Stout, but this is an excellent example of a dessert-inspired beer that knows when to stop.
