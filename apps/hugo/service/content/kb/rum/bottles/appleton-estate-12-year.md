# Why I Bought It

After spending the last year focused almost exclusively on bourbon, I wanted to broaden my understanding of aged spirits.

I had previously enjoyed Appleton Estate Signature Blend, but it had been quite some time since my last pour. My memory was of a sweeter, more approachable rum.

Appleton Estate 12 Year Rare Casks offered an opportunity to revisit Jamaican rum through a more mature expression and compare it against the bourbon profiles I've come to appreciate.

The question I wanted to answer:

**Can an aged Jamaican rum deliver the same depth and complexity that draws me to bourbon while still offering its own identity?**

# Appearance

Deep amber with attractive copper highlights.

Excellent clarity and slow, oily legs suggest a richer body than many entry-level rums.

---

# Nose

The nose immediately surprised me.

Compared to what I remembered from Signature Blend, this felt quieter and more restrained.

Instead of bright sweetness, I found:

- Aged caramel
- Burnt sugar
- Oak
- Dark fruit
- Sugarcane
- A subtle Jamaican funk

The oak influence stood out much more than expected.

---

# Palate

Easy sipping with a smooth, approachable character.

The profile is noticeably different from bourbon, but there are familiar elements.

I found:

- Dark caramel
- Burnt sugar
- Dried fruit
- Sugarcane
- Toasted oak

Unlike bourbon, the spice is very subdued.

Instead of corn sweetness and baking spices, the rum leans toward molasses, oak, and tropical fruit.

---

# Finish

Medium length.

The sweetness slowly fades while oak and dark fruit linger.

The Jamaican character remains present without becoming overpowering.

---

# Mouthfeel

A richer mouthfeel than I expected.

Not quite as viscous as some barrel-proof bourbons, but it carries enough body to feel substantial without being heavy.

---

# What I Liked

The biggest surprise was how mature and restrained it felt.

Rather than overwhelming me with sweetness, the barrel influence played a much larger role.

I also appreciated:

- Better oak integration than I remembered from Signature Blend
- Balanced sweetness
- Smooth sipping experience
- Distinct identity without trying to imitate bourbon

---

# What I'd Change

At this point, nothing obvious.

This is my first serious exploration into aged Jamaican rum, so I'd rather spend more time with the bottle before deciding what I would change.

---

# Food Pairings

I'd like to try this with:

- Grilled pineapple
- Smoked pork
- Jerk chicken
- Bread pudding
- Dark chocolate
- Pecan pie

---

# Where It Fits

This doesn't replace bourbon.

Instead, it fills a different role.

When I want oak, caramel, and complexity without the baking spice and higher proof of bourbon, Appleton Estate 12 fits naturally.

It feels more like an alternative than a substitute.

---

# Final Thoughts

The biggest takeaway wasn't the rum itself—it was realizing how much my palate has evolved.

When I first tried Appleton Estate Signature Blend, sweetness stood out the most.

After spending the past year exploring bourbon, I now notice oak, barrel influence, mouthfeel, and balance first.

Appleton Estate 12 reflects that change. It's less about sweetness and more about integration and maturity.

It reminds me of why I enjoy bottles like Woodford Reserve Double Oaked and Old Forester 1910—not because they taste the same, but because they share a similar emphasis on oak and dark caramel while expressing it in completely different ways.
