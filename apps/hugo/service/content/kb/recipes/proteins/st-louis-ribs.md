# 🥩 St. Louis Style Ribs Runbook

> **Objective:** Produce repeatable St. Louis style ribs with deep smoke flavor, well-developed bark, a clean bite, and consistent tenderness by making decisions based on the condition of the meat—not the clock.

---

# Purpose

This runbook is designed to consistently produce ribs with:

- Deep smoke penetration
- Rich mahogany bark
- Tender but structured bite
- Clean bone separation
- Balanced seasoning
- Repeatable results across cooks

---

# Equipment

## Smoker

- Traeger Woodridge Pro

## Fuel Strategy

### Primary Smoke Phase

Use premium pellets while the meat is actively accepting smoke.

Recommended:

- Smokin' Pecan
- B&B Championship Blend

### Heat Phase

After wrapping, smoke absorption has largely finished.

Switch to economical pellets for heat production.

Recommended:

- Kirkland Premium Blend

This preserves premium pellets for the portion of the cook where they have the greatest impact while reducing overall operating cost.

---

# Meat Preparation

## Trim

- Remove membrane from the bone side.
- Square edges if desired.
- Remove loose fat and meat.
- Preserve uniform thickness where practical.

---

## Dry Brine (Recommended)

Apply kosher salt evenly.

Preferred:

- Refrigerate uncovered overnight.

Minimum:

- 4 hours before cooking.

Dry brining improves seasoning penetration, bark development, and moisture retention.

---

# Seasoning

Apply seasoning after the dry brine.

Suggested profile:

- Black pepper
- Paprika
- Garlic powder
- Onion powder
- Light brown sugar
- Optional chili powder

Allow seasoning to hydrate for approximately 20–30 minutes before placing the ribs on the smoker.

---

# Smoker Setup

## Initial Temperature

225–250°F

Recommended:

- 225°F during the primary smoke phase for maximum smoke exposure.
- Increase to 250–275°F after wrapping to finish rendering.

## Rack Placement

- Bone side toward primary heat source.
- Leave space between racks for airflow.

## Super Smoke

Use only during the primary smoke phase.

Disable after wrapping since additional smoke provides minimal benefit.

---

# Cooking Philosophy

This procedure is **decision driven**, not time driven.

Internal temperature is used as a reference—not as the primary decision point.

Every major decision should be validated by visual inspection and physical tenderness testing.

---

# Phase 1 — Smoke & Bark Formation

Maintain 225°F.

Do not frequently open the smoker.

Primary objectives:

- Build bark
- Develop smoke flavor
- Begin fat rendering

## Exit Criteria

Proceed only when:

- Bark has developed to a deep mahogany color.
- Rub no longer smears when touched.
- Surface moisture has largely evaporated.
- Fat is visibly beginning to render.

Ignore elapsed cook time.

---

# Phase 2 — Bark Development & Rendering

Increase temperature if desired to 250°F.

Continue cooking until:

- Meat begins pulling back from the bones.
- Bark is fully established.
- Bend test begins showing flexibility.

Temperature remains secondary to appearance.

---

# Phase 3 — Wrapping Decision

Wrap when **the cook—not the thermometer—is ready.**

Decision criteria:

✔ Bark fully set

✔ Rub no longer smears

✔ Desired bark color achieved

✔ Bend test beginning to loosen

✔ Probe resistance noticeably decreasing

Typical internal temperature:

**165–175°F**

Temperature confirms readiness.

It does **not** determine readiness.

---

# Wrapping

Preferred:

- Pink butcher paper

Alternative:

- Heavy-duty foil

Butcher paper:

- Better bark preservation
- More breathable
- Less steaming

Foil:

- Faster cooking
- Softer bark
- More braising effect

---

# Phase 4 — Finish

Maintain approximately:

250–275°F

Continue cooking until tenderness is achieved.

Ignore the clock.

---

# Tenderness Evaluation

Internal temperature is only a checkpoint.

Primary indicators are:

## Toothpick Test (Primary)

Insert a wooden toothpick between the bones.

Properly finished ribs should offer only slight resistance—similar to inserting the toothpick into warm butter.

## Bend Test

Lift the rack near the center.

Properly finished ribs should:

- Bend significantly
- Begin cracking slightly on the surface
- Not completely break apart

## Bone Pullback

Bones should have visible exposure without excessive shrinkage.

Use all three evaluations together before pulling the ribs.

---

# Glazing Strategy

Two validated finishing methods are available.

---

## Method A — Traditional

Glaze before wrapping.

Wrap.

Rest.

Produces:

- Softer bark
- Glaze integrated into the bark
- Slightly richer exterior

---

## Method B — Late Glaze (2026 Validation)

Wrap first.

Cook until nearly finished.

Unwrap.

Apply glaze.

Return to the smoker for approximately 20 minutes.

Allow glaze to set before removing.

Rest.

Produces:

- Firmer bark
- Better bark preservation
- Excellent glaze adhesion

Initial testing showed outstanding results.

Continue evaluating across future cooks before declaring a permanent standard.

---

# Rest

Remove from the smoker once tenderness is confirmed.

Rest in butcher paper.

Allow the ribs to rest until aggressive surface steaming has stopped.

Typical rest:

20–30 minutes.

Resting is considered part of the cook—not an optional step.

---

# Slice

Turn ribs bone-side up.

Slice cleanly between each bone.

Maintain structural integrity.

Avoid crushing the bark while slicing.

---

# Serving

Serve immediately after slicing.

Optional finishing:

- Light glaze
- Additional finishing rub
- Dry presentation emphasizing bark

Avoid excessive sauce that hides the smoke profile.

---

# Health Checks

Before serving, verify:

✅ Bark is dark and fully set

✅ Rub remains adhered

✅ Bones have slight pullback

✅ Bend test passes

✅ Toothpick slides easily

✅ Meat remains juicy

✅ Bite is clean without falling completely from the bone

---

# Lessons Learned

## Temperature Is Guidance

Internal temperature is useful for tracking progress but should never override tenderness testing.

---

## Bark Must Be Earned

Do not wrap simply because a thermometer reaches a certain number.

Wrap only after the bark has fully developed.

---

## Smoke Early

Premium smoke pellets provide the greatest value during the first half of the cook.

After wrapping, pellet selection primarily affects heat production rather than smoke flavor.

---

## Validate with Multiple Tests

Never rely on a single indicator.

Use:

- Visual inspection
- Bend test
- Toothpick test
- Bone pullback
- Internal temperature

Together they provide the most reliable assessment.

---

# Derrick's Rules

- Cook to tenderness—not time.
- Bark is earned, not rushed.
- Temperature is a guide—not the finish line.
- Validate every major decision visually before trusting the thermometer.
- Lid stays closed unless there is a reason to open it.
- Every cook is a calibration run.
- Record lessons while they're fresh.
- Improve the process one cook at a time.
