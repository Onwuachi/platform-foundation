# 🥃 Derrick's Bourbon Journey

> *"This started as trying to find the best bourbon.
>
> It became learning why I liked certain bourbons more than others."*

---

# Introduction

About a year ago I decided I wanted to actually learn bourbon.

Not just drink it.

Understand it.

Like many newcomers, I thought the goal was simple:

Find Buffalo Trace.

Find Blanton's.

Find Weller.

Find whatever YouTube said was impossible to find.

Like many new enthusiasts, I assumed rarity automatically meant quality.

Fortunately...

I was wrong.

Over dozens of bottles, hundreds of pours, countless comparisons, and more side-by-side flights than I can remember, something much more rewarding happened.

I stopped chasing bottles.

I started building a palate.

Today, I care much less about whether a bourbon is allocated.

I care far more about whether it makes me smile every time I pour a glass.

That change completely transformed bourbon from a collecting hobby into a learning hobby.

---

# The Evolution of My Palate

Looking back, my bourbon journey naturally fell into several stages.

Each bottle taught me something different.

## Stage 1 — Learning the Basics

Every bourbon drinker starts somewhere.

Mine began with approachable, affordable bottles.

These weren't exciting because they were rare.

They were exciting because they taught me what bourbon actually tasted like.

### Bottles

- Evan Williams Bottled in Bond
- Wild Turkey 101

At the time my vocabulary was simple.

Smooth.

Hot.

Sweet.

Strong.

I didn't yet know how to describe oak, mouthfeel, spice, or finish.

These bottles gave me my first reference points.

---

## Stage 2 — Discovering Balance

As I explored more shelves, I discovered bottles that felt richer and more complete.

Elijah Craig Small Batch became one of my first "wow" bourbons.

Four Roses Small Batch introduced a softer fruit-forward style.

Bowman Brothers Small Batch showed another expression of traditional Kentucky bourbon.

For quite a while, Elijah Craig Small Batch represented what I thought good bourbon should taste like.

Eventually my palate moved beyond it—but it deserves recognition because it raised the bar.

---

## Stage 3 — Chasing Proof

Like many enthusiasts, I eventually became fascinated with proof.

Higher proof had to mean better...

Right?

This stage introduced some incredible bottles.

- Wild Turkey Rare Breed
- Russell's Reserve Single Barrel
- Russell's Reserve 10 Year
- Old Forester 1910
- Old Forester 1920
- Elijah Craig Barrel Proof
- 1792 Full Proof

Ironically, this stage taught me that proof wasn't actually the goal.

Some of the highest-proof bottles drank easier than lower-proof bottles.

Rare Breed was one of the biggest surprises.

Despite its proof, it rarely felt harsh.

Instead it was bold, flavorful, and remarkably balanced.

Proof became another characteristic to appreciate—not a score to chase.

---

## Stage 4 — Discovering Mature Bourbon

This stage completely changed my buying habits.

Instead of asking:

> "What's the hottest bottle?"

I began asking:

> "Which bottle feels the most complete?"

This stage introduced bottles like:

- Heaven Hill Bottled in Bond 7 Year
- Henry McKenna Bottled in Bond
- Knob Creek 9
- Knob Creek 12
- Knob Creek Blender's Edition 01
- Bardstown Bottled in Bond

These bottles taught me that mature oak doesn't have to be bitter.

Older bourbon wasn't simply more oak.

It was often better integrated.

Richer.

More balanced.

More interesting.

This is also where Bottled in Bond became something I actively looked for when shopping.

---

## Stage 5 — Discovering Dessert Bourbon

Woodford Reserve Double Oaked changed how I thought about oak.

Before this bottle, I associated heavy oak with bitterness.

Instead I found:

Rich caramel.

Chocolate.

Toffee.

Dark raisin.

Toasted oak.

Most importantly...

Oak that was present without becoming bitter.

That simple observation would later become one of the defining characteristics of my palate.

---

# The Biggest Lesson

If I could summarize everything I've learned over the past year into one sentence, it would be this:

> **The best bourbon isn't the rarest bottle.
>
> It's the bottle that best matches your own palate.**

That sounds obvious now.

It wasn't obvious when I started.

Originally I believed bourbon was about finding consensus.

Today I believe bourbon is about finding yourself.

The internet can recommend bottles.

Friends can recommend bottles.

Reviewers can recommend bottles.

But eventually every bourbon drinker reaches a point where they discover something unique.

Their own preferences.

Mine turned out to be surprisingly consistent.

---

# My Flavor DNA

After dozens of bottles and countless comparisons, I've learned the characteristics that almost always make me happy.

## Always Wins

✓ Rich caramel

✓ Peanut brittle

✓ Mature oak

✓ Baking spices

✓ Cinnamon

✓ Dark cherry

✓ Orange peel

✓ Thick, oily mouthfeel

✓ Long finish

✓ Balanced sweetness

✓ Integrated pepper

✓ Chocolate

✓ Toffee

✓ Leather

---

## Usually Loses

Young harsh oak

Thin mouthfeel

Sharp ethanol

Overly grassy rye

Artificial cherry sweetness

Excessive herbal notes

One-dimensional sweetness

Oak bitterness

Flavors that feel disconnected rather than integrated

---

# Integration Over Intensity

This has become the defining philosophy of my palate.

When I first started drinking bourbon, I thought bigger always meant better.

Higher proof.

More oak.

More spice.

More intensity.

Today I almost think the opposite.

The bottles I consistently return to aren't necessarily the loudest.

They're the bottles where every flavor works together.

Heaven Hill Bottled in Bond.

Russell's Reserve Single Barrel.

Woodford Double Oaked.

Even Kirkland Signature Bottled in Bond surprised me because the pepper wasn't aggressive—it was integrated into the bourbon.

I've discovered I don't necessarily reward intensity.

I reward harmony.

When every note supports every other note, the bourbon becomes greater than the sum of its parts.

That realization changed how I evaluate every bottle I open.
