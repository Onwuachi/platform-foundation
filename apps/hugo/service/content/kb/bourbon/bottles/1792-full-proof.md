# About 1792 Full Proof

Unlike "barrel proof," **Full Proof** means the bourbon is bottled at the same proof it entered the barrel—125 proof—rather than the proof at which it exited the barrel after aging.

It's a subtle but interesting distinction that gives this expression its identity within the 1792 lineup.

---

# Appearance

Rich amber with copper highlights.

The whiskey clings heavily to the glass, suggesting a full-bodied pour.

---

# Nose

The nose is bold but inviting.

I found:

- Caramel
- Brown sugar
- Vanilla
- Toasted oak
- Dark fruit
- Cinnamon
- Black pepper

The oak is immediately more prominent than the sweetness.

---

# Palate

This drinks like a serious high-proof bourbon.

The first sip delivers:

- Rich caramel
- Brown sugar
- Vanilla
- Toasted oak
- Dark cherry
- Baking spice
- Black pepper

The proof brings excellent concentration without overwhelming the palate.

The oak takes center stage more than the sweetness, giving the bourbon a drier profile than some of my favorites.

---

# Finish

Long and warming.

Oak remains the dominant note, accompanied by black pepper and baking spice.

One characteristic I consistently noticed is a lingering bitter oak note that appears late in the finish.

It never becomes unpleasant, but it's enough to keep this from reaching the very top tier of my collection.

---

# Mouthfeel

Rich and oily.

The higher proof provides an excellent texture that coats the palate nicely.

---

# Overall Impression

1792 Full Proof is an impressive bourbon.

It delivers everything I expect from a quality high-proof pour: concentration, body, and a long finish.

Its oak-forward personality makes it feel mature and robust, though the slight bitterness on the finish keeps it just below my personal favorites.

---

# What I Liked

- Excellent proof integration
- Rich mouthfeel
- Big oak character
- Long finish
- Strong value for a high-proof bourbon
- Well-balanced sweetness

---

# What I'd Change

I would dial back the lingering bitter oak slightly.

A touch more caramel richness or dark fruit on the finish would create better balance.

---

# Comparison Notes

## Compared to Larceny Barrel Proof

These two occupy a similar space in terms of proof and intensity.

### Larceny Barrel Proof

- Sweeter
- Creamier
- More caramel
- Softer spice
- Wheated richness

### 1792 Full Proof

- More oak
- More pepper
- Drier finish
- More barrel-forward
- Slight bitter oak note

If I want richness, I lean toward Larceny Barrel Proof.

If I want a more traditional oak-driven bourbon, 1792 Full Proof gets the nod.

---

## Compared to Old Forester 1920

Both are outstanding high-proof bourbons.

1920 delivers greater complexity and richer dessert notes.

1792 Full Proof emphasizes oak, structure, and spice.

# Final Thoughts

1792 Full Proof is an excellent example of what Barton Distillery can produce.

It combines high proof with impressive balance, rich texture, and traditional Kentucky bourbon flavors.

The lingering bitter oak on the finish keeps it just outside my personal top tier, but there's no denying the quality in the glass.

It's a bourbon I'd confidently recommend to anyone looking to explore high-proof expressions beyond the usual Wild Turkey and Brown-Forman offerings.
