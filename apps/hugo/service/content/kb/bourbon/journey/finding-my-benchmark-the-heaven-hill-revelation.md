# The Heaven Hill Revelation

More than any other discovery, this changed how I think about bourbon.

When I first started learning, I assumed my benchmark bourbon would eventually come from Buffalo Trace.

After all, that's what everyone seemed to talk about.

Allocated bottles.

Waiting lists.

Secondary prices.

Hype.

Instead, something unexpected happened.

I kept coming back to one bottle.

Heaven Hill Bottled in Bond 7 Year.

Every time I opened it, I found myself thinking the same thing.

"This is exactly what I want bourbon to taste like."

It wasn't the oldest bottle I owned.

It wasn't the highest proof.

It certainly wasn't the hardest to find.

It simply delivered everything my palate enjoys.

Rich caramel.

Peanut brittle.

Mature oak.

Balanced cinnamon spice.

An oily, coating mouthfeel.

A finish that lingered without becoming bitter.

Over time, it quietly became my benchmark.

Every new bottle now gets compared against Heaven Hill 7 Year Bottled in Bond.

Not because it's objectively the "best" bourbon.

Because it's the bourbon that best matches my palate.

That realization completely changed how I shop.

Instead of asking,

> "Is this better than Heaven Hill?"

I started asking,

> "Does this offer something different from Heaven Hill?"

That's a much healthier—and more enjoyable—way to explore bourbon.

# Discovering Bottled in Bond

Learning about the Bottled in Bond Act of 1897 completely changed the way I browse liquor store shelves.

Before, "Bottled in Bond" was just another phrase on the label.

Now it's one of the first things I notice.

A Bottled in Bond bourbon must:

- Be produced by one distillery.
- Be distilled during a single distilling season.
- Be aged at least four years.
- Be bottled at exactly 100 proof.
- Be aged in a federally bonded warehouse.

None of those requirements guarantee a great bourbon.

But together they establish a high baseline for consistency and quality.

Over time I found myself intentionally seeking out Bottled in Bond expressions.

Not because every one would become a favorite.

Because they consistently represented excellent value and a great way to learn each distillery's house style.

Today, some of my favorite bottles all happen to carry that designation.

# The DSP Number Rabbit Hole

One of the most enjoyable rabbit holes I've fallen into has been learning about Distilled Spirits Plant (DSP) numbers.

At first they looked like meaningless government markings.

Then I realized they tell part of a bourbon's story.

For example:

DSP-KY-1

The Bernheim Distillery in Louisville.

Modern Heaven Hill distillation.

DSP-KY-12

Heaven Hill's historic Bardstown operations.

Seeing these numbers helped me understand that two bottles carrying different DSP numbers aren't necessarily made by different companies—or even from different recipes.

They simply reflect where the bourbon was distilled, bottled, or otherwise produced according to federal labeling requirements.

Once I started paying attention to DSP numbers, bottles became much more than labels.

They became pieces of a much larger story about Kentucky bourbon.

# Distillery Fingerprints

Another lesson surprised me.

Distilleries have personalities.

Even before learning mash bills, I found myself recognizing certain house styles.

## Heaven Hill

Rich caramel.

Peanut brittle.

Balanced oak.

Excellent Bottled in Bond offerings.

Generally the style I return to most often.

Representative bottles:

- Heaven Hill 7 Year Bottled in Bond
- Henry McKenna Bottled in Bond
- Evan Williams Bottled in Bond
- Elijah Craig Small Batch
- Elijah Craig Barrel Proof

---

## Wild Turkey

Bolder.

Rustic.

More cinnamon.

More rye spice.

Dark fruit.

The famous "Turkey funk."

Representative bottles:

- Wild Turkey 101
- Rare Breed
- Russell's Reserve 10 Year
- Russell's Reserve Single Barrel

These bottles taught me that proof and drinkability are not the same thing.

Rare Breed, despite its proof, often drinks more gracefully than many lower-proof bourbons.

---

## Brown-Forman

Dessert.

Chocolate.

Bananas Foster.

Marshmallow.

Rich toasted oak.

Representative bottles:

- Old Forester 1910
- Old Forester 1920
- Woodford Reserve Double Oaked

These bottles expanded my understanding of oak and sweetness.

Woodford Double Oaked, in particular, showed me that oak could be intense without becoming bitter.

---

## Jim Beam

Nutty.

Peanut brittle.

Classic bourbon sweetness.

Excellent value.

Representative bottles:

- Knob Creek 9 Year
- Knob Creek 12 Year
- Knob Creek Blender's Edition 01

These bottles proved Beam makes much more than everyday shelf bourbon.

Older Knob Creek expressions became some of the best values I've purchased.

# Bottles That Shaped My Palate

Looking back, some bottles stand out not because they became favorites, but because they taught me something new.

## Wild Turkey 101

My introduction to Wild Turkey's house style.

Excellent value.

Still one of the best bottles under $30.

---

## Elijah Craig Small Batch

The first bottle that made me realize bourbon could have depth beyond sweetness.

It eventually got surpassed, but it deserves credit for raising my expectations.

---

## Four Roses Small Batch

Introduced a softer, fruitier style than many of the bourbons I'd tried.

It expanded my understanding of how different mash bills and yeast recipes can influence flavor.

---

## Bowman Brothers Small Batch

A reminder that approachable, well-balanced bourbon doesn't have to be expensive or highly allocated.

---

## Bardstown Bottled in Bond

One of my earliest Bottled in Bond discoveries.

Months later, when I opened Kirkland Signature Bottled in Bond, my first thought was,

"This reminds me of Bardstown."

That kind of subconscious comparison was a sign my palate had started building lasting flavor memories.

---

## Henry McKenna Bottled in Bond

Honey.

Vanilla.

Elegant sweetness.

Bottle variation is part of the experience, but when it's good, it's excellent.

---

## Old Forester 1910

The bottle that introduced me to dessert bourbon.

Marshmallow.

Chocolate.

Rich toasted oak.

---

## Old Forester 1920

Dark cherry.

Chocolate.

Brown sugar.

Viscous texture.

A perfect example of how higher proof can still feel balanced.

---

## Elijah Craig Barrel Proof

Powerful.

Dense.

Complex.

A reminder that big bourbon can remain elegant when well made.

---

## 1792 Full Proof

A very different interpretation of full-proof bourbon.

Rich and flavorful without simply trying to overwhelm the palate.

---

## Bondstone Double Oak Cask

An interesting take on double-oaked bourbon.

It reinforced how much I enjoy oak when it's integrated rather than bitter.

Each of these bottles contributed something unique to my understanding of bourbon.

Some became favorites.

Others became teachers.

Both are equally valuable.
