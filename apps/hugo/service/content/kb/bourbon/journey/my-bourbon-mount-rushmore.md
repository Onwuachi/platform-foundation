# My Bourbon Mount Rushmore

If every bottle on my shelf disappeared tomorrow, these are the first four I'd replace.

Not because they're objectively the best bourbons ever made.

Because they've earned permanent places in my cabinet.

---

## 🥇 Heaven Hill Bottled in Bond 7 Year

**The Benchmark**

This bottle quietly changed everything.

It isn't flashy.

It isn't allocated.

It isn't difficult to find.

Yet every time I compare another bourbon against it, I find myself asking:

> "Does this actually improve on Heaven Hill?"

Most don't.

Flavor profile:

• Peanut brittle

• Rich caramel

• Mature oak

• Cinnamon

• Thick oily mouthfeel

• Long balanced finish

This is my North Star.

---

## 🥈 Russell's Reserve Single Barrel

**The Elegant Bourbon**

If Heaven Hill is comfort...

Russell's Reserve Single Barrel is sophistication.

Dark cherry.

Orange peel.

Leather.

Elegant oak.

Beautiful spice.

Everything feels intentional.

Nothing dominates.

It reminds me of drinking a perfectly built Old Fashioned without any added sugar.

This bottle showed me what refinement tastes like.

---

## 🥉 Wild Turkey Rare Breed

**The Wild One**

Rare Breed has personality.

Brown sugar.

Cinnamon.

Turkey funk.

Heavy oak.

Power.

Yet somehow...

It rarely drinks as hot as its proof suggests.

Rare Breed taught me that proof and drinkability are not the same thing.

It remains one of the best barrel-proof values available.

---

## 🏅 Woodford Reserve Double Oaked

**Dessert Bourbon**

This bottle surprised me more than almost any other.

Chocolate.

Toffee.

Vanilla.

Dark raisins.

Heavy toasted oak.

Most importantly...

Oak without bitterness.

Buying the 1.75-liter bottle officially made this a house bourbon.

# Rare Breed vs Russell's Reserve Single Barrel

Few comparisons have taught me more than this one.

Both come from Wild Turkey.

Both are excellent.

Yet they scratch completely different itches.

## Rare Breed

Wild.

Rustic.

Brown sugar.

Heavy cinnamon.

Powerful oak.

Turkey funk.

Controlled chaos.

Every sip feels exciting.

---

## Russell's Reserve Single Barrel

Dark cherry.

Orange peel.

Leather.

Elegant spice.

Balanced oak.

Long finish.

Everything feels composed.

Where Rare Breed is a campfire...

Russell's Reserve Single Barrel is a jazz club.

I genuinely love both.

Choosing between them depends entirely on my mood.

# The Budget Bourbon Revelation

Perhaps the biggest surprise of my bourbon journey has been discovering just how good inexpensive bourbon has become.

Some of my favorite pours cost under $40.

Several cost under $30.

That realization completely changed how I buy bourbon.

Instead of asking,

"What costs the most?"

I now ask,

"What delivers the most enjoyment?"

---

## Heaven Hill 7 Year Bottled in Bond

Still my benchmark.

Around $40.

Worth every penny.

---

## Wild Turkey 101

One of the best values in American whiskey.

Classic Wild Turkey.

Reliable.

Always available.

---

## Evan Williams Bottled in Bond

Traditional Heaven Hill.

Caramel.

Toffee.

Pepper.

Dependable.

A bottle that consistently overdelivers for its price.

---

## Kirkland Signature Bottled in Bond

Perhaps the biggest surprise of the year.

At roughly twenty-five dollars for a liter...

It had no business being this enjoyable.

# Evan Williams BiB vs Kirkland Signature BiB

This comparison completely surprised me.

Originally I assumed Kirkland would simply taste like Evan Williams.

Instead...

It developed its own identity.

## Appearance

### Evan Williams

Slightly darker amber.

### Kirkland

Slightly lighter amber.

---

## Nose

### Evan Williams

Caramel.

Pepper.

Toffee.

Traditional mature bourbon.

### Kirkland

More floral.

Lighter.

Sweeter.

Slightly younger.

---

## Palate

### Evan Williams

Classic Heaven Hill.

Caramel.

Balanced pepper.

Integrated oak.

Dependable.

---

### Kirkland

Creamier.

Sweeter.

Less pepper.

More floral.

Better integrated than expected.

The biggest surprise wasn't that Kirkland tasted inexpensive.

It didn't.

Instead, it reminded me more of Henry McKenna Bottled in Bond—and even brought back memories of Bardstown Bottled in Bond.

That's not something I expected.

# The Evolution of My Tasting Notes

One of the most satisfying parts of this journey has been watching my own vocabulary evolve.

Early notes looked like this.

"Smooth."

"Hot."

"Sweet."

Today they look more like this.

Integrated pepper.

Creamy mouthfeel.

Oily texture.

Mature oak.

Young oak.

Dark cherry.

Caramel.

Toffee.

Leather.

Orange peel.

Long finish.

Balanced sweetness.

That's probably the biggest sign that I've actually learned something.

I'm no longer trying to decide whether a bourbon is "good."

I'm trying to understand why it tastes the way it does.

That's a much more interesting question.
